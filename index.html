02 Literature Context

Five Papers. Five Things I Actually Learned.

  • 01
    Classical pairs papers show edge erosion under realistic costs — transaction costs must be deducted inside each simulated trade, not subtracted as a lump sum at the end. Do & Faff (2010) tracked monthly excess returns falling from ~1.44% to ~0.40% over 40 years as markets matured.
  • 02
    Cointegration is not permanent — structural breaks kill it quickly — rolling pair-health checks are needed every trading day, not a one-time check at training. Assuming the 2015 relationship still holds in 2023 is exactly the mistake this framework catches.
  • 03
    Regime-switching models are most useful for cutting drawdown, not boosting turnover — Nystrup et al. (2017) applied hidden Markov models to portfolio optimisation. The lesson: regime filters work best as gates that say "don't trade now," not signals that say "trade more."
  • 04
    Adaptive hedging only helps when the relationship is drifting, not fully breaking — Clegg & Krauss (2018) showed Kalman-estimated parameters help only when drift is real. If cointegration is gone, a filter that keeps updating just chases noise.
  • 05
    False discovery is the biggest risk in multi-pair research — Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) showed most reported factor Sharpes don't survive proper multiple-testing correction. So every result here gets walk-forward folds, bootstrap confidence intervals, Monte Carlo null comparisons, and a false-discovery-rate correction across all 60 pairs simultaneously.
The framework's core question
"Is this pair tradeable right now?"
Not six months ago. Not in training. Today.

Three live checks on every trading day — ADF stationarity · Hurst exponent · Engle–Granger cointegration — on a rolling 126-day window. At least 2 of 3 must pass before any capital is committed.

A deep RL agent was trained separately on a 9-dimensional state space — z-score, regime label, Hurst exponent, health score, current position, rolling P&L, and more. Three actions: long, short, or flat. No instruction to prefer staying flat.

On all four pairs tested, it independently learned to hold 100% flat. Not because we told it to — because active trading produced worse outcomes than doing nothing. 05 My Honest Assessment

What I Want You to Actually Walk Away With

What I'm proud of
  • Scale matters: I moved from a single hand-picked pair to 60 pairs across 10 sectors with no per-pair tuning. The risk improvement holds across all of them — that's not a one-off result.
  • I caught my own mistakes: my earlier Sharpe ratios of 1.57–2.74 were inflated by in-sample tuning and post-hoc cost subtraction. I fixed the methodology, documented the drop, and kept going.
  • The drawdown number is real: 34.39% median max drawdown cut to 8.73% — confirmed by a rank-based test (p < 10⁻¹²) and independently replicated by an RL agent using a completely different method.
  • Everything is reproducible: paper, figures, tables, and scripts are consistent. The pipeline runs in 4 minutes on a single laptop. All code is public on GitHub.
What I'm being honest about
  • Alpha is hard to generalise: filtered median Sharpe across all 60 pairs is still negative. PNC/CFG and FCX/VMC look promising — but "promising" and "proven" are different things.
  • The filter trades coverage for safety: a pair that's healthy only 7–21% of the time barely trades. Fewer windows means less data, and confidence intervals include zero for every pair including the best ones.
  • After false-discovery correction, 0 of 60 pairs cross the significance bar: PNC/CFG at p = 0.031 is genuine signal worth investigating — but one pair at p = 0.031 out of 60 candidates isn't a settled finding.
  • Five weeks with one trade proves infrastructure, not strategy: the pipeline runs end-to-end. That was the goal. Production performance requires months more observation.
